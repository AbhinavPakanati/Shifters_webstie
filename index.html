Our Mission

Why This Matters

Social media has become the primary source of news for millions of young adults — yet it's also the biggest breeding ground for misinformation. We set out to understand the human experience behind fake news: the confusion, the emotion, the habits that make misinformation spread.

A social media user who relies on online platforms for news needs to have a fast, simple, and trustworthy way to verify information before sharing it — because they want to stay informed quickly without accidentally spreading misinformation or damaging their credibility.

How might we help users quickly evaluate the credibility of online information so that misinformation sharing decreases and responsible digital consumption becomes a natural habit?

Define Phase

Problem Statement Canvas

Using the integrated design thinking framework, we mapped our user, their context, pain points, root causes, and the scale of impact to arrive at a clear, actionable problem definition.

👤

User / Protagonist

  • Young adults aged 18–25
  • Heavy social media users
  • Platforms: Instagram, X/Twitter, TikTok, WhatsApp, YouTube
  • Consume news quickly, struggle to verify before sharing
🌐

Context & Situation

  • Viral or trending posts
  • Breaking news events
  • Emotionally charged headlines
  • AI-generated videos, images, text
  • Peer pressure to stay updated
  • Fast-paced digital environment
📋

Evidence Base

  • Users trust viral posts due to share count
  • Most do not verify before reposting
  • Fact-checking seen as time-consuming
  • AI-generated content harder to detect
  • Empathy mapping & user journey research
⚡

Pain Points & Needs

  • Difficulty identifying trustworthy information
  • Lack of quick verification tools
  • AI-generated media increasingly realistic
  • Information overload from multiple platforms
  • Fear of sharing fake information publicly
🔎

Root Causes (5 Whys)

  • Social media prioritizes engagement over accuracy
  • Users consume information too quickly
  • Fact-checking tools are disconnected from browsing
  • Emotional headlines encourage impulsive reactions
  • Social pressure encourages rapid reposting
📊

Impact & Scale

  • Rapid spread of misinformation
  • Public confusion and panic
  • Manipulation of opinions
  • Loss of trust in news
  • Damage to personal credibility
  • Increased polarization and distrust

Social media is now the dominant source of news for young adults — overtaking TV, newspapers, and official channels entirely.
Users frequently feel confused about whether news is real or fake, especially when content is emotionally charged or widely shared.

🤖 The AI Amplification Problem

AI plays a significant role in worsening fake news. Since most people already lack confidence identifying misinformation, AI-generated content makes this worse by producing highly realistic articles, images, and videos indistinguishable from genuine information. AI also enables rapid, large-scale distribution through automated bots — and can manipulate social engagement through fake accounts, making false information appear credible. The result: greater confusion, stronger emotional impact, and a more profound influence on opinions and decision-making.

Users say they care about sharing accurate information — but in practice they do repost news without verifying. They value truth but prioritize convenience and speed.

Core Insight

Social media users rely on headlines or viral posts instead of verified sources. Many want to stay informed but lack easy tools. Misinformation spreads quickly because users share content before verifying it.

Proposed Solution

TruthShift — Our Final Solution

Synthesizing everything we learned — from 40 survey responses, multiple in-depth interviews, empathy maps, journey mapping, and 144 brainwriting ideas — we propose TruthShift: an integrated digital ecosystem that meets users where they already are and makes verification the path of least resistance.

TruthShift

Shift the default. Verify before you share.

TruthShift is a browser extension and mobile overlay that integrates seamlessly into any social media platform. Before a user shares a post, TruthShift runs a real-time credibility check — pulling from verified fact-check databases, analyzing AI-generation signals in text and media, surfacing the account's misinformation history, and presenting a simple Trust Score with a one-sentence summary. The goal is not to block sharing — it's to make one extra second of informed reflection the new default behavior.

🔍

Pillar 1 — Real-Time Credibility Layer

Every post gets a live Trust Score the moment you hover or tap share — powered by AI credibility scoring and fact-check database matching.

AI-based credibility score (0–100)
Auto-match with 12+ fact-check archives
Source verification badge system
Metadata authenticity analysis
One-sentence plain-English summary
🤖

Pillar 2 — AI & Deepfake Detection

As AI-generated content becomes indistinguishable from real media, TruthShift provides passive detection that flags manipulation before it spreads.

Deepfake facial inconsistency scanning
AI-written text pattern detection
Manipulated audio waveform analysis
Digital authenticity watermark checks
Manipulation probability alert overlay
🎯

Pillar 3 — Habit Engine

Behavior change requires more than tools — it requires positive reinforcement. The Habit Engine turns verification into a rewarding, identity-building practice.

Pause-before-share prompts ("Are you sure?")
Verification streaks and badges
Daily fact-check micro-challenges
Post-correction learning feedback
Media literacy progress tracking

Reflection & Learning

What We Learned

Design thinking is as much about the journey as the solution. Here is what Team Shifters learned — about the problem, about our users, and about ourselves as designers and researchers.

01

Empathy Changes Everything

Going into interviews, we assumed fake news was mostly a media literacy problem. Talking to real users showed us it's equally an emotional and social problem — people share misinformation because they're anxious, pressured, or just want to feel connected. Understanding this reframed our entire solution approach.

Emotional triggers drive sharing more than knowledge gaps
Social pressure to share trending news is underestimated
People feel genuine anger and shame when deceived
02

The AI Problem Is Already Here

We expected to find concern about future AI-generated media. Instead, participants were already living it — deepfakes of celebrities had fooled family members, AI articles had changed real opinions. Our solution had to address this as a present-tense crisis, not a future risk.

Deepfakes are already affecting real communities, not just headlines
Users know AI content exists but have no tools to detect it
Trust in all video content is eroding rapidly among young users
03

Friction Can Be a Feature

Early in ideation, we wanted to make fact-checking invisible and seamless. User feedback helped us understand that a small, intentional pause — a moment of friction — is actually valuable. The 3-second overlay in TruthShift isn't an obstacle; it's a design choice that respects the user's desire to do the right thing.

Users want to verify — they just need an easy way to do it
Behavioral nudges work better than hard blocks
Designing for intention, not just action, matters
04

Research Changes Your Solution

We generated 144 ideas across 24 HMW questions. Without structured research, we would have built a simple fact-check tool. The interview insights, empathy maps, and journey mapping revealed that habit-building, emotional design, and AI detection were equally important — leading us to TruthShift's three-pillar architecture.

Quantity of ideas protects against falling in love with the first one
Journey mapping revealed Stage 4 (regret) as a key underserved moment
The 6-3-5 method forced ideas from every team member equally
05

Team Collaboration Lessons

Nine people, multiple interview schedules, and a shared document of 24 HMW questions taught us real lessons about collaborative design. Division of labor based on strengths (interviewing, analysis, ideation, documentation) made the process efficient — and every disagreement sharpened our thinking.

Assigning roles by strength, not availability, improved output quality
WhatsApp group kept audio recordings and transcripts organized in real time
Creative conflict in ideation led to better ideas
Documentation discipline is a design skill
06

What We Would Do Differently

With more time and resources, we would prototype and test TruthShift with real users — running A/B tests on the pause overlay timing, testing different reward structures for the Habit Engine, and validating the Trust Score UX with a broader demographic including older social media users.

Prototype earlier — even a paper prototype would have surfaced UX questions
Interview more diverse age groups (parents, 35–55) who are highly vulnerable
Test the Habit Engine reward system with a real cohort
Quantify the AI detection false-positive rate before deployment
